English Translation:  
Now that all the family members have married and established their own households, I no longer need to worry about them. Later, I asked relatives and friends for help—such as my cousin’s wife (“xīn niáng pó,” a Hokkien term for a newlywed or young daughter-in-law), whose mother-in-law lives nearby; locals call her “Jiāngxī Pó” (Aunt from Jiangxi). At that time, I proactively sought assistance—specifically, through an introduction by my paternal aunt.  

Lǐ Qiūjǐng, aged ninety-three, and Dù Què, aged eighty-four, both hail from Jìnjiāng, Fujian Province. After World War II, they migrated to Singapore to join their father. From the 1950s through the 1970s, they worked as manual laborers—carrying heavy cargo along the Singapore River.  

While working, people urged each other: “Quick—get up! Start moving, get to work!” I’d then call the children, and they’d immediately prepare to rise and begin. Though we’re nearly eighty, if we stumble, we steady ourselves, gather our composure, and press on—however difficult it is, we encourage one another to keep working.  

The money I earned in Singapore was all sent home to support my younger brother’s education and his pursuit of scholarly advancement (the phrase “gāo péng guà kē” appears to be a phonetic misstatement; given context and Hokkien influence, it likely means “supporting my brother’s academic studies and civil service examinations”).  

Daily necessities—rice, household goods, toothpaste, etc.—were cooked in large pots. If transported in “gāo zǎi” containers (possibly a local term or mispronunciation for “large jars” or “barrels”), a single cartload could bring them in—but jostling en route often caused spillage or even complete loss. Sometimes, payments were settled via card (“shuā kǎ”) or formal accounting.  

These medicine jars are mostly “bā wǎ guàn” (eight-liang jars); some hold eight units, others are cupping jars (mistakenly called “Brazilian cupping jars” due to phonetic similarity), and some are chipped or cracked—only eight intact jars can be used. Excess jars aren’t useful, as they dry out and crack over time.  

When packing, I’d climb atop the load while you stood here, placing ten planks (or strips) on top for stability. Goods were stacked up to the “yáng” level (likely a mispronunciation of “shàng” [top] or “yàng” [platform]); the topmost layer—the “bāo tóu” (binding at the head/top)—was secured right there. Then came mixed loading, reinforcement, and careful final inspection.  

Look how it’s arranged—here, we perform a ritual bow. Bowing itself follows strict custom: after bowing, we may speak freely and give instructions. Once the final tasks (“wěi liào”) are completed, the job is truly finished—no rework is needed, nor any further service provided.  

Well then—everything worth saying has been said. I’m suddenly thirsty, so I’ll take a sip of water first; if anything I say next is imprecise, please forgive me.  

In those days, “Dà Gū Boats” shuttled up and down the Singapore River. Green-painted bows indicated ownership by Fujianese crews; solid red bows belonged to Teochew operators. Riverfront docks were likewise divided by dialect group: crossing into another group’s territory—or competing for business—often triggered social ostracism and sometimes escalated into mass brawls among fellow provincials.  

Hurry—turn around! Oh—and five positions oversee economic affairs: roughly three to four hundred people are involved; about three hundred are currently assigned, with approximately fifty vacancies (“wǔ líng sì” likely means “fifty-four,” but context suggests “around fifty”). “Líng sì” probably refers to “Group Four”; I was supposed to lead it—I’d signed up to serve as coach—but they didn’t invite me, nor did they make an exception for me.  
I asked them: “Are these five posts secured because of my connections with them?” They replied, “We’re afraid too!”—repeating “afraid” several times. Afraid of what? It turned out they feared being supervised by the very person who’d just spoken up.
